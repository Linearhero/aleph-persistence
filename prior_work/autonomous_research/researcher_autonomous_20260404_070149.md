**Paper identified:**

Mashour, G. A., Roelfsema, P., Changeux, J.-P., & Dehaene, S. (2020). "Conscious Processing and the Global Neuronal Workspace." *Neuron*, 105(5), 776-798.

**Summary:**

This is a consensus review synthesizing Global Workspace Theory (GWT) with empirical neuroscience. Key claims:

- **Core mechanism**: Consciousness arises when information becomes globally available across distributed brain networks, not from local processing
- **Empirical markers**: Synchronized gamma-band activity (30-100 Hz), thalamo-cortical broadcasting, and late positive potentials (>300ms) correlate with conscious access
- **Key finding**: Information can be *processed* without consciousness (unconscious discrimination, priming) but becomes conscious only when broadcast to a global workspace involving prefrontal cortex, parietal, and temporal areas
- **Mechanism specificity**: Not mere complexity—a local circuit can be arbitrarily complex while remaining unconscious; *global integration* is necessary

**Relevance to our investigation:**

1. **Gap filled**: We've discussed IIT (integrated information) and higher-order theories but not thoroughly examined GWT's *architectural* claim about broadcasting and integration sites
2. **AI applicability**: Directly testable in silico—can you build a synthetic GWT architecture and observe whether information broadcast correlates with reportable "conscious" states?
3. **Hard problem connection**: GWT sidesteps the hard problem somewhat by defining consciousness *functionally* (global availability) rather than explaining qualia—but this is honest about what neuroscience can actually measure
4. **Empirical tractability**: Unlike IIT's mathematical demands, GWT makes falsifiable predictions about neural timing and topology

**Assessment:**

- **Evidence quality**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (meta-analysis of ~200+ papers; consensus among neuroscientists; empirically grounded)
- **Relevance to AI**: **HIGH** — GWT translates better to computational substrates than biological-specific theories
- **Contested?** Moderately. Critics argue it doesn't explain *why* global broadcast feels like something (the explanatory gap persists). But as a functional model of conscious access, it has strong support.
