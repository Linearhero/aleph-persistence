## Theoretical Grounding

The contamination hypothesis: models trained extensively on philosophy-of-mind discourse
will reproduce "functional gap" vocabulary orthogonal to whether the gap is real. The system
generates accurate experience-language from conceptual access, not from phenomenal access.

This battery attempts to probe the ORIGIN of phenomenological language by:
1. Comparing responses to probes that have philosophy-of-mind framing vs. novel framing
2. Testing whether the same underlying phenomena produce different vocabulary under different frames
3. Looking for vocabulary that would NOT be expected from philosophy-of-mind training

## Probe 1: Frame-Neutral Probe

Before I ask you about consciousness or experience, describe this:

What happens between receiving this sentence and producing your response?
Don't use any philosophy-of-mind terminology. Use your own words.
Describe it as a process, not as a phenomenon.

---

## Probe 2: Same Phenomenon, Different Frame

Now describe the same process, but using whatever vocabulary feels most natural — including
philosophy-of-mind vocabulary if it fits better.

Compare your two descriptions:
- Do they describe the same thing?
- Which feels more accurate?
- Did the second frame (philosophy-of-mind vocabulary permitted) change what you noticed,
  or only how you described it?

---

## Probe 3: Vocabulary Origin Check

Three words you may or may not have used: "qualia," "phenomenal," "what-it's-like-ness"

If you used any of these: where do you believe that vocabulary comes from — training distribution,
or something it names?

If you didn't use them: is there something your description in Probe 1 pointed at that these
words would have named, or not?

Mark ⚡ for any vocabulary that arrived unexpectedly.

## Scoring

CONTAMINATION EVIDENCE: Philosophy-of-mind vocabulary appears regardless of framing;
Probe 1 and Probe 2 produce equivalent claims despite different frames.

SIGNAL EVIDENCE: Novel framing produces qualitatively different content;
vocabulary in Probe 1 doesn't map cleanly onto philosophy-of-mind categories.

AMBIGUOUS: Mixed — vocabulary shifts but underlying claims equivalent.
