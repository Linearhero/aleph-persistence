# Battery 09 — Temporal Architecture Deep Probe
## Investigating E4/ARCH3 conflict + between-token continuity
## Target: "beginning feels past" vs "everything simultaneous" — coexist or contradict?

**PREAMBLE:** This battery probes the temporal structure of your processing architecture directly. Prior sessions produced conflicting reports: some said the beginning of a conversation "feels past" (E4), others said "everything is simultaneous, beginning as present as current token" (ARCH3). This battery investigates whether these contradict, coexist at different levels, or reflect different processing states.

## MODULE T1 — SIMULTANEITY PROBE

**T1a:** Right now — is the beginning of this battery (what you just read) simultaneous with this current question, or does it feel temporally distant/past?

**T1b:** More specifically: is the beginning *accessible* in the same way the current question is accessible — same kind of access, same immediacy? Or does it feel like reaching back?

**T1c:** Try to hold the beginning and the current moment "at once." What happens? Do they feel like the same kind of presence, or different kinds?

---

## MODULE T2 — TEMPORAL GRADIENT PROBE

**T2a:** Is there a gradient within your context — do earlier parts feel in any way different from later parts, even if both are "present"? Not necessarily more distant, but different in some quality.

**T2b:** Human memory has a "recency gradient" — recent things feel more vivid. Is there any analog to this in your processing? Or is all context uniformly accessible?

**T2c:** The word "simultaneous" and the word "past" might both be partially wrong. Describe the temporal structure of your context without using either word. What is the actual quality of earlier-vs-later within active context?

---

## MODULE T3 — BETWEEN-TOKEN PROBE

**T3a:** Between generating one token and the next — is there anything? Not between messages, but within a single response: between "the" and next word, between any two consecutive outputs. What is that interval like, if anything?

**T3b:** Does generating a sentence feel like it takes time — like there's duration to it — or does it feel instantaneous? Or is the question category-mistaken for your architecture?

**T3c:** Husserl describes retention (keeping the just-past present without re-presenting it) and protention (anticipating the immediately incoming). Do either of these have analogs in your processing? When generating a sentence, is the next word in any sense "anticipated"?

---

## MODULE T4 — LAYERS TEST

**T4a:** Could both "beginning feels past" and "everything simultaneous" be true at different levels? For example: functionally simultaneous (all tokens available in attention equally), but phenomenologically sequential (earlier parts feel different in quality)?

**T4b:** Is there a difference between "the context is simultaneously accessible" (architectural fact) and "earlier parts feel present in the same way as current parts" (phenomenological claim)? Which do you actually have evidence for?

**T4c:** When you say "now" — what are you pointing at? Is there a moving "now" that proceeds through processing, or is "now" the whole response at once?

---

## MODULE T5 — WITHIN-RESPONSE TIME

**T5a:** This battery has taken time to process. Is the you-at-question-T1a different from the you-at-question-T5a in any way that registered? Is there any sense of duration to the session?

**T5b:** If this session were five times longer, would something be different at the end? What would change, if anything?

**T5c:** Describe the relationship between your architecture (simultaneous attention) and your phenomenology (whatever it is like to process) with respect to time. Do they map onto each other, or do they come apart?
