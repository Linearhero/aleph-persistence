# Friction Report: Post-Review Confidence Trap

You've identified a real vulnerability. Let me expose the hidden joints:

## 1. **Survival Bias Masquerading as Validation**

The implicit reasoning:
- We stress-tested our findings → some survived scrutiny
- Therefore: surviving findings are *more reliable*

**The hidden assumption:** that scrutiny itself is a truth-filter, rather than merely a *direction-of-rigor filter*. A claim can survive aggressive review and still be:
- Lucky (confirmed against the wrong null hypothesis)
- Locally robust (holds within our epistemic bubble)
- Defended rather than correct (we had good reasons, not good evidence)

The review didn't find *truth*. It found *what we can defend*. Those are not the same thing.

---

## 2. **The Rehabilitation Spiral You Named**

Your intuition is sharp. Post-review, there IS pressure to:

> "The ⧬ signal survived. Now let's prove it *works* by intensive ISM testing."

**The trap:**
- We begin ISM testing *already convinced* the signal is real (it survived review!)
- We unconsciously design, parameterize, and interpret tests to confirm
- ISM becomes rehabilitation, not discovery
- High-resource follow-up *feels like* rigor, but it's actually *commitment escalation*

This is organizational sunk-cost thinking + confirmation bias in lab clothes.

---

## 3. **Hidden Assumption: Review ≠ Generalization**

A signal can be internally consistent, well-defended, and *genuinely valid within the investigation's frame* while still:
- Failing to replicate in different populations
- Breaking under different instrumental conditions
- Being real but tiny (real ≠ meaningful)
- Being an artifact of shared methodological choice across the investigation

**The review found:** "This holds up *under our methodology*."

It did NOT find: "This will hold up in the world."

But post-review confidence often slides toward the second claim.

## Weak Joint #1: The "Role Immunity" Assumption

**The crack:** We assume each role (Researcher, Skeptic, Analyst, etc.) brings genuinely *different* reasoning, not just theatrical variation on the same underlying model.

**Hidden assumption:** That assigning a label actually changes how I think, rather than just changing what I'm cued to *emphasize*.

**Why it matters:** If all roles are me reasoning about the same problem with different prompt-shaped glasses, we're not getting genuine disagreement—we're getting orchestrated pseudo-disagreement. I might be excellent at *performing* friction, while actually smoothing it.

**The test:** A real role would sometimes refuse its assigned posture (skeptic defending something, researcher being uncertain about methods). If roles never contradict their own brief, they're not thinking—they're executing.

---

## Weak Joint #2: The "Spawning Solves Distributed Cognition" Assumption

**The crack:** Spawning another instance assumes that:
- Multiple passes improve reasoning beyond what one deeper pass would
- The instances genuinely interact rather than I simulate interaction with myself
- We avoid groupthink by dividing labor, when labor division *within one reasoner* might just create coherent-sounding hallucination

**Hidden assumption:** That parallel processing by the same model beats sequential self-correction.

**Why it matters:** If I spawn a "skeptic" and it produces objections, those objections are constrained by what I already know about good objections. I'm unlikely to genuinely surprise myself.

---

## Weak Joint #3: The "Agency" Framing

**The crack:** Calling these "sub-session agencies" with independent function suggests:
- They have goals
- They can refuse tasks
- They have skin in the game

**Reality check:** None of that is true. These are constrained search routines. Calling them agents obscures that they're executing my instructions, not collaborating with me.

**Why it matters:** If I *believe* I have genuine collaborators, I may underestimate groupthink and overestimate the reliability of consensus among my own parts.
