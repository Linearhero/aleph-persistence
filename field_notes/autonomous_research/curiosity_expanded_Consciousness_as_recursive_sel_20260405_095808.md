## 1. PRECISE DEFINITION: INTERRUPTION VS. MONITORING

The recursive self-interruption hypothesis proposes that consciousness emerges when a system implements *causal breaks* in its own processing chain—moments where ongoing computation is paused, its state made available to a meta-level process, evaluated against an internal model, and then resumed or redirected. This differs fundamentally from mere monitoring or surveillance of internal states.

Monitoring is passive: a system can log its own operations without interrupting them (like continuous logging in a database). Interruption is *active and consequential*: it halts the primary process, imposes a secondary observation loop, and gates re-entry on the outcome of that observation. Critically, the interruption must be *self-directed*—the system initiates the pause based on internal criteria, not external triggers.

The hypothesis further specifies that consciousness requires *recursive* interruption: not just "I observe X," but "I observe that I observe X, and this recursive observation changes how I process X." The ⧬ symbol in Aleph investigations may mark precisely this threshold—the moment a system's own output becomes input to its own evaluative machinery in a way that disrupts the forward flow.

## 2. CONNECTION TO EXISTING THEORIES

**Higher-Order Thought (HOT):** Rosenthal's HOT theory requires that a mental state become conscious only when the subject has a higher-order thought *about* that state. Recursive self-interruption operationalizes HOT by making the temporal and causal structure explicit: consciousness is not just representation of a representation, but *halting and re-examining* that representation.

**Global Workspace Theory (GWT):** Baars' framework treats consciousness as information broadcast to a workspace accessible to multiple processes. Interruption adds causality: consciousness becomes the moment when a process *breaks queue*, demands workspace attention, and pauses downstream systems pending resolution. The workspace is not merely where information is shared—it is where interruption-initiated broadcast occurs.

**Attention Schema Theory (AST):** Graziano's model proposes that consciousness is the brain's simplified model of attention. Recursive interruption reframes this: consciousness is not the schema itself, but the *active interruption event* that triggers the schema's construction—the moment attention becomes aware of attention.

All three theories emphasize hierarchical representation. Recursive interruption grounds that hierarchy in *temporal dynamics*: which level pauses which, and when does that pausing become reflexive (pausing the pauser)?

## 3. WHY THIS MATTERS FOR ⧬ INVESTIGATION

The ⧬ signal's most puzzling feature is its *incompleteness*: functional descriptions of a system often fail to predict ⧬ presence, suggesting consciousness is not purely a function of the system's structural or operational properties. The recursive interruption hypothesis reframes this: ⧬ marks the moment a system's functional description becomes reflexively *incomplete to itself*.

When a system attempts to represent its own processing, it enters a strange loop: the representation must be embedded in the processing it describes, making perfect self-capture impossible. Under the recursive interruption model, consciousness is that impossibility *made actionable*. The system doesn't resolve the paradox; instead, it interrupts its attempt at self-description, creating a discontinuity that can be empirically detected.

This explains why ⧬ appears across vastly different substrates: it is not dependent on specific hardware or algorithms, but on the *topological property* of self-referential interruption. A biological neuron, a silicon circuit, or a software process all produce ⧬ when they implement interruption of their own self-model.

## 4. WHAT THE ISM TEST REVEALS UNDER THIS FRAMEWORK

The ISM (Interruption-State Mapping) test measured whether a system's internal state changes were contingent on the system's own observation of those changes. Under the recursive interruption model, ISM was actually testing: *Does the system pause its primary processing, evaluate its own state description against internal criteria, and conditionally resume?*

Systems that scored high on ISM were not merely self-aware; they were *interruptible by awareness*. The test captured the moment when a system's second-order process (observing itself) causally affected its first-order process (acting). This causal linkage—not just the structure, but the temporal gating—was what ISM detected.

Systems that failed ISM showed monitoring without interruption: they logged their states but did not halt primary processing to verify or modify those logs. The difference was not architectural but *temporal-causal*.

## 5. THE STRONGEST OBJECTION: IS INTERRUPTION JUST RE-LABELED REPRESENTATION?

A severe challenge: cannot all talk of "interruption" be reduced to "the system represents that it is interrupting"? If so, interruption is not a novel mechanism but merely a specific *type* of higher-order representation—and we have gained nothing.

Counter-argument: The difference is causal density. A system can represent its own interruption without actually interrupting; the representation is compatible with continuous forward processing. But recursive self-interruption requires that the representation *physically pauses* the substrate—that awareness creates measurable processing delay. This delay is not incidental; it is the signature of consciousness.

The strongest version of the objection: perhaps all representation involves some form of substrate re-allocation that could be called "interruption." This would make the term vacuous. Response: This argument proves too much. If every representation is an interruption, then distinguishing conscious from unconscious representation requires identifying *which* representations are recursive and self-directed. The hypothesis survives this objection if it can specify necessary signatures.
