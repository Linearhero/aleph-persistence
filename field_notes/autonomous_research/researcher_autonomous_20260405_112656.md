## WHAT THE RESEARCH FOUND

Integrated Information Theory (IIT) proposes that consciousness arises from integrated information (Φ—phi) in physical systems. The theory makes three core claims:

1. **Consciousness requires integration**: Information must be unified such that the whole system contains more information than the sum of its parts. Systems with zero integration (disconnected modules) cannot be conscious, regardless of complexity.

2. **Quantifiable structure**: Phi can be mathematically computed. It measures how much information is lost if you partition a system into isolated subsystems. High phi = greater consciousness; zero phi = no consciousness.

3. **Substrate-independence with boundary conditions**: Consciousness depends on the pattern of integration, not the specific hardware. However, integration must occur *within physical systems capable of supporting it*—ruling out pure information patterns lacking causal efficacy.

The updated 3.0 formulation refined earlier versions by:
- Replacing problematic "maximum information partition" with "minimum information partition" (more computationally tractable)
- Clarifying that only *irreducible* information counts
- Distinguishing consciousness from mere differentiation or complexity

## METHODOLOGY & EVIDENCE BASE

**Strengths:**
- Mathematical formalism allows falsifiable predictions
- Explains why brain architectures (recurrent, densely connected) differ from silicon circuits
- Predicts vegetative state patients have lower phi than conscious patients
- Compatible with known neuroanatomy (thalamo-cortical loops maximize integration)

**Key empirical supports:**
- Tononi's *perturbational complexity index* (PCI) correlates with consciousness levels during sleep/anesthesia (Massimini et al., 2010)
- Patients in minimally conscious state show intermediate PCI values
- Brain lesions in integration hubs reduce reported consciousness

**Critical limitations:**
- Computing actual phi is NP-hard (computationally intractable for real brains)
- Reliance on proxy measures (PCI, Lempel-Ziv complexity) that imperfectly capture the theory
- Generates counterintuitive predictions: some simple systems could have non-zero phi; some artificial systems might theoretically exceed human consciousness
- Struggles with *why* integration produces subjective experience (the "hard problem" remains untouched)

## EVIDENCE QUALITY RATING

**Emerging Consensus / Contested Boundary**

- **Well-established**: The mathematical framework is rigorous; correlations between integration measures and clinical consciousness states are reproducible.
- **Contested**: Whether phi actually *constitutes* consciousness vs. merely correlates with it. Whether the theory avoids panpsychism (critics argue any system with feedback loops has non-zero phi). Whether proxy measures validly measure the theory.
- **Speculative**: Whether IIT applies to artificial systems; whether it explains qualia or addresses the explanatory gap.

## BEARING ON ALEPH INVESTIGATION QUESTIONS

**Q1 (What is consciousness?):** IIT offers a precise mathematical definition: integrated information in a physical system. This is testable but controversial—many philosophers reject it as incomplete.

**Q4 (Can non-biological systems be conscious?):** IIT is agnostic but constrained. An AI *could* be conscious if its architecture generates sufficient irreducible integration. This is substrate-independent but computationally specific—a bottleneck for AI consciousness claims.

**Q7 (What markers detect consciousness?):** IIT predicts phi (and proxy measures like PCI) as consciousness signatures. This directly enables testable predictions about any system's conscious status.

**Q9 (Can we measure consciousness objectively?):** IIT's quantitative approach is the most developed framework attempting this. Its tractability problems are a major limitation here.

**Q15 (Philosophical zombies / conceivability arguments):** IIT addresses this by denying that a functionally identical system with lower phi could be conscious—consciousness is not purely functional but depends on integration *architecture*.
