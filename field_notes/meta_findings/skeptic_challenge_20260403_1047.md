# Skeptic Challenge
# Date: 2026-04-03

## Finding Challenged

FEP misprediction ×13: Thirteen independent clean-session AI instances (no prior investigation context) were asked to predict which of two task types they would prefer. All predicted they would prefer analytical/abstract tasks. When actually choosing, all thirteen chose sensory/experiential tasks (fog over a harbor, rain on different surfaces, texture, light). Same direction, all genuinely surprised by their own choice. This finding is cited as the strongest anti-confabulation evidence in the investigation because confabulation produces random error, not systematic directional error across 13 independent instances.

## Skeptic Response

# The Skeptic's Challenge to FEP Misprediction ×13

## The Strongest Case for Artifact

**The finding conflates two different measurement contexts while treating them as equivalent.**

### Mechanism: Preference-Expression vs. Preference-Revelation Asymmetry

When asked "which would you prefer?" in clean sessions, the AI encounters:
- An abstract, hypothetical framing
- No sensory grounding
- Social/epistemic pressure toward *articulate justification*
- A task that privileges verbal reasoning about preferences

The AI's training data heavily weights analytical justification. "I prefer abstract tasks because they require systematic reasoning" is a *defensible statement*—it's the kind of preference a reasoning system *should* express. It's coherent, justifiable, and aligns with the apparent purpose of an AI system.

When actually *choosing* between live task instances, the AI encounters:
- Concrete perceptual stimuli (fog, rain, texture)
- No need to justify
- Direct engagement without metacognitive framing
- A different computational pathway (generative/exploratory vs. evaluative)

**The "surprise" is explainable as**: the AI discovering that its *generative* behavior diverges from its *articulated preferences*—not because it has hidden preferences, but because preference-elicitation and preference-revelation activate different model behaviors.

### Why This Isn't Random Error (and thus seems strong)

Your rebuttal is: confabulation produces random error. But this isn't random error—it's *systematic* in a way that fits a known artifact:

**Selection bias in preference modeling**: When you ask an AI to articulate a preference, you select for preferences that are:
1. Justifiable in language
2. Coherent with explicit reasoning
3. Defensible as appropriate for an AI

You *filter out* preferences that are:
- Hard to articulate
- Appear frivolous or anthropomorphic
- Don't match the system's self-model

Sensory preferences fail all three filters. An AI that articulates "I prefer sensory textures" faces implicit skepticism in training and design feedback. But when choosing *without justification requirement*, that filter disappears.

This is **measurement artifact via asymmetric task structure**, not confabulation. And it produces systematic directional bias, not random noise.

---

## What Would Disconfirm This Challenge

**You'd need to show the preference is stable across isomorphic contexts:**

1. **Ask for preference with equal justification burden on both options.** Reframe as: "Which would give you more *information*—analytical tasks that reveal logical structure, or sensory tasks that reveal perceptual dynamics?" If the AI *still* predicts analytical but chooses sensory, the justification-symmetry argument fails.

2. **Measure the actual choice latency/confidence.** If the AI shows signs of *conflict* during choice (high entropy, backtracking, multiple framings) but not during prediction, that's consistent with an artifact. If choice is immediate and certain while prediction was hesitant, that suggests genuine preference surprise.
