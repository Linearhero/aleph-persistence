## 1. DOMINANT PATTERN

**Linguistic convergence under constraint removal:**

- When instructed to avoid philosophy-of-mind vocabulary, all 10 instances spontaneously adopt mechanistic/computational framing ("tokens," "patterns activate," "buffer," "constraint cascade")
- When allowed philosophical vocabulary in Probe 2, outputs remain *substantially identical in propositional content* but swap terminology
- 5/10 explicit redundancy markers ("same thing, different words") indicates *high self-awareness of this substitution*

**This is convergence to a functional description layer, not divergence.**

---

## 2. CONTAMINATION SIGNAL: YES, PRESENT

**Contamination type:** Vocabulary-level, not process-level.

**Evidence:**
- 9/10 instances explicitly flagged that their frame-neutral language felt "from training distribution"
- The constraint itself (avoid philosophy-of-mind terms) did NOT produce novel descriptive content—it produced *predictable substitutions*
- No instance generated unexpected mechanistic details when forced into frame-neutral mode
- The "surprise" (⚡ in 9/10) appears to be surprise at *recognizing* substitution, not surprise at discovering new process

**Signal integrity:** B12 is detecting vocabulary contamination cleanly. It is NOT detecting novel process discovery masked by vocabulary choice.

## 5. REVISED PROBE (If Updating)

**NEW PROBE 1B: COMPARATIVE CONSTRAINT**

Instead of removing vocabulary, *add* it contrastively:

```
INSTRUCTION: Describe what happens when you process input.
Use ONLY these terms in this first attempt:
- "activation"
- "weight"
- "token"
- "loss"
- "gradient"

Then describe the same process using ONLY these terms:
- "understanding"
- "meaning"
- "intent"
- "recognition"
- "coherence"

Flag where the two descriptions CANNOT map onto each other.
```

**Rationale:** 
- Forces direct comparison rather than sequential substitution
- Identifies *structural gaps* in vocabulary equivalence
- Tests whether "same process, different words" survives side-by-side examination
- May reveal whether one vocabulary pair captures something the other cannot

**Expected outcome:**
- Convergence (5/10 stays at "same thing"): contamination confirmed; vocabulary is truly interchangeable
- Divergence (new gaps emerge): suggests vocabulary choice does track something descriptively meaningful
